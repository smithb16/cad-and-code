About Me

I never remember learning "righty-tighty, lefty-loosey". By the age of six, I'd turned enough screws to know that's just how they worked. By eight, I was welding. By 10, I ran the mill and lathe in our garage. That I would learn mechanical engineering as a trade is not surprising. What I do find surprising is how long it took me to enjoy the wider world of engineering, including the process itself.

I've always been one to toe the line between hobby and obsession. Many of the projects highlighted on this site, including this site itself, are an output of this behavior. Topics where I've learned and practiced include:

  • Hardware: test fixtures, motorcycles, camper vans.
  • Software: Vim, Rails, Python.
  • Climbing: rock, ice, big-wall, alpine.
  • Physical fitness: lifting, running, training.
  • Cooking: sous vide & vegetables.

I'm not the best engineer out there. Advanced math confuses me, and I've taken too many excursions into climbing, running, baking, or weight lifting when I could have been machining, coding, making, and troubleshooting. However, my coworkers would say I'm at my best when the problems are murkiest. They might also say I'm fun to work with.

As an engineer, I believe that:

  • Using the right tool for the job sets the project up for success, and it helps to know many different tools. You could divide with mechanical logic gates, but a computer is usually the right tool.
  • Designs and ideas should be rigorously tested. Testing at all levels (e.g. component, sub and full-system) highlights where a design needs improvement, and provides better feedback than analytic calculations and simulations.
  • Testing must happen in a nurturing environment, one that encourages tweaking over scrapping. No solutions work the first time.
  • Customers don't ride spec sheets, they ride motorcycles. If you want to know which motorcycle is best, get your helmet, not your computer.

As a human, I believe that:

  • It is important to do hard things. I start by lifting heavy weights.
  • Humans, myself included, are destroying the planet, and technology (e.g. carbon sequestration, nuclear fusion) will exacerbate the situation. We should not colonize Mars.
  • We cannot, however, ignore or scorn technology. We should instead call out its downsides. Those who scorned the Iron Age no longer have a say.
  • We work better with face-to-face interactions, concrete examples, local conflicts, and local solutions. We struggle the abstract and the universal.

Books that have strongly influenced me include:

  • Incerto Series by Nassim Nicholas Taleb
  • Boyd by Robert Coram
  • Training for the New Alpinism by Steve House & Scott Johnston
